The Problem This Solves

Three wealth-killers that interrupt compounding momentum

Tax Drag

Selling winners to fund lifestyle triggers capital gains, estate, and ordinary income taxes‚Äîpermanently reducing your capital base.

Liquidation events crystallize long-term capital gains (23.8% federal max), state taxes, and potential NIIT exposure. Estate inclusion subjects assets to 40% federal estate tax above exemption thresholds.

Forced Selling

Needing cash forces you to sell at the market's timing, not yours‚Äîoften during downturns when recovery matters most.

Liquidity needs during market dislocations force pro-cyclical asset sales at depressed valuations, impairing long-term compounded annual growth rate (CAGR) and sequence-of-returns risk.

Compounding Interrupted

Every dollar extracted is a dollar that can't compound. Over decades, the opportunity cost is massive.

Withdrawals reduce principal subjected to exponential growth. At 8% annual return, $1M withdrawn at age 50 represents ~$10M+ in foregone terminal wealth by age 80.

Premium Financing (Optional): Funding Premiums With a Bank Facility

Plain English: A lender may fund a portion of premiums via a loan. Client posts collateral and pays interest (usually current). This can reduce upfront out-of-pocket cash but adds leverage and introduces collateral-call and rate risks.

Technical: Premium financing facility provides debt capital to fund PPLI premiums (typically 70-90% of premium). Borrower posts collateral (liquid securities, real estate, or policy itself after sufficient cash value buildup). Interest typically paid current; capitalization increases risk profile. Loan structured as interest-only with balloon maturity (commonly 5-10 years) or amortizing. Lender may require minimum FICO, net worth, liquid assets. Covenants include LTV maintenance, collateral value maintenance, timely interest payment.

Benefits

  • Interest may be deductible (consult tax advisor)
  • Can increase policy size / death benefit

What Can Go Wrong

  • Under-delivery: Policy growth < loan cost = negative arbitrage
  • Lender repricing/covenants: Terms can change; refinance risk
  • Balloon risk: Large repayment due at maturity

How It Fails‚ÄîAnd What We Do

Transparency first. Here's what can go wrong, how the guardrails respond, and when we pull the kill switch.

Under-Delivery Risk

What happens: Investment returns fall short of projections. Spread cushion shrinks or goes negative.

Technical: Realized net policy return (g_actual) < projected (g_assumed). Spread cushion (g - r) compresses. If cushion < buffer (e.g., <2%), automatic PAUSE on new borrowing.

Guardrail Response:
  • Auto-pause new borrowing when spread < buffer
  • Reassess investment strategy and assumptions
  • Rebuild reserves or consider partial de-lever

Drawdown Risk

What happens: Market crash causes policy value to drop sharply (e.g., -20% or worse). LTV spikes.

Technical: Severe negative return (e.g., -20%+) reduces Pt significantly while Lt remains constant. LTV = Lt / Pt increases. May approach hard cap or trigger covenant breach.

Guardrail Response:
  • FREEZE all new borrowing immediately
  • Preserve liquidity reserves for interest payments
  • Monitor collateral adequacy; prepare cure options
  • Wait for recovery; avoid forced selling at depressed levels

Rate Spike Risk

What happens: Reference rates (SOFR, etc.) jump higher. Your loan cost increases, spread cushion shrinks.

Technical: If reference rate increases +200 bps, all-in rate r increases proportionally. Spread cushion (g - r) compresses. If r > g, negative arbitrage condition.

Guardrail Response:
  • Auto-pause if spread < buffer (prevents digging deeper)
  • Increase reserve to cover higher interest cost
  • Explore rate hedging or loan renegotiation
  • Consider partial repayment to lower interest burden

Carrier / Lender Risk

What happens: Policy carrier rating downgrade, lender changes terms, or availability disruption.

Technical: Carrier financial strength rating downgrade may trigger policy replacement considerations. Lender covenant changes or credit line reduction may require refinancing or delever.

Guardrail Response:
  • Use only highly-rated carriers (A+ or better recommended)
  • Maintain diversified lender relationships where feasible
  • Annual review of carrier and lender health
  • Have contingency refinancing plan

Compliance / Tax Law Risk

What happens: Tax laws change. IRC ¬ß7702 rules modified. Policy loses favorable tax treatment.

Technical: Congressional changes to IRC ¬ß7702 (life insurance definition), ¬ß101(a) (death benefit exclusion), or new anti-abuse provisions could alter tax efficiency or require policy restructuring.

Guardrail Response:
  • Annual tax/legal review with qualified advisors
  • Monitor legislative changes and IRS guidance
  • Conservative structuring minimizes aggressive positions
  • Restructuring options if laws change materially

Personal Liquidity Risk

What happens: You can't pay current interest due to personal cashflow disruption.

Technical: Income loss, unexpected expenses, or other cashflow shock prevents timely interest payment. Forced to capitalize interest (PIK), accelerating loan balance and LTV growth.

Guardrail Response:
  • Mandatory 12‚Äì24 month interest reserve provides buffer
  • Reserves allow time to resolve cashflow issues
  • Options: tap reserves, partial policy loan, or de-lever
  • Avoid PIK if possible‚Äîcompounds debt problem

What is PPLI and why use it?

Private Placement Life Insurance (PPLI) is a tax-advantaged insurance wrapper that lets you invest in diversified assets while growth compounds tax-deferred (and death benefit is potentially tax-free). It's designed for accredited investors seeking tax efficiency.

What happens if I can't pay the interest one year?

Your 12‚Äì24 month interest reserve is designed for this scenario. You tap reserves to cover payments temporarily. If reserves run low, you must rebuild them or consider partial loan repayment. Avoid capitalizing interest (PIK) if possible‚Äîit accelerates debt growth.

What if the market crashes 30% in one year?

The drawdown trigger (e.g., -20%) would activate, freezing all new borrowing. You'd continue paying interest from reserves but stop increasing leverage. The goal: preserve liquidity and wait for recovery rather than forced selling at the bottom. If LTV approaches the hard cap, you may need to post additional collateral or de-lever.

Severe drawdown scenario (e.g., -30% return) reduces Pt significantly. Example: $5M policy ‚Üí $3.5M post-crash. IRC ¬ß817(h) Diversification Requirements

Understanding Diversification Compliance (Plain English)

What is IRC ¬ß817(h)?

IRC ¬ß817(h) is a tax code provision that requires variable life insurance and annuity contracts to maintain adequate diversification of their underlying investments. This ensures that the insurance policy is not being used primarily as an investment vehicle, but rather maintains its insurance character for tax purposes.

Treasury Regulation ¬ß1.817-5

The implementing regulations (Treas. Reg. ¬ß1.817-5) provide the specific rules, tests, and safe harbors for determining whether a policy's separate account is adequately diversified. Key aspects include:

  • Diversification tests that limit concentration in any single investment
  • Look-through rules that may apply to underlying funds or vehicles
  • Safe harbor provisions for certain types of investments
  • Quarterly testing requirements to ensure ongoing compliance
  • Asset class limitations (generally, no more than 55% in one asset class, no more than 70% in two asset classes, no more than 80% in three)

Why This Matters

Tax Treatment Depends on Compliance: If a policy fails to maintain adequate diversification, it may lose its favorable tax treatment under IRC ¬ß7702 and ¬ß101(a). This could result in:

  • Loss of tax-deferred growth inside the policy
  • Current taxation on policy earnings
  • Loss of income-tax-free death benefit treatment
  • Potential penalties and interest

Founding Member Charter

This Charter defines the terms, capacity caps, pricing, scorecard objectives, fee credit/waiver policy, and disclosures for Ekantik Capital Advisors' Founding Member soft launch.

1. PURPOSE

This Charter establishes the framework for Ekantik Capital Advisors LLC's ("ECA") Founding Member program. Founding Members receive early access to ECA's proprietary investment strategies (ECFS and EPIG 500) with locked pricing, defined scorecard objectives, and fee credit/waiver protection for a limited capacity.

2. CAPACITY CAPS

Founding Member status is limited by two caps:

  • Member Cap: Maximum 25 Founding Members
  • AUM Cap: Maximum $100,000,000 in aggregate AUM across all Founding Members

Once either cap is reached, Founding Member status closes. New entrants after capacity is filled may face different pricing and/or terms at ECA's discretion.

3. ELIGIBILITY

Founding Member candidates must be accredited investors (as defined by SEC Rule 501 of Regulation D) with substantial liquid net worth (typically $5M+ liquid, $10M+ net worth) and the operational capacity to engage qualified tax and legal advisors. Acceptance is at ECA's sole discretion and subject to suitability assessment.

4. SCOPE OF SERVICES

Founding Members receive:

  • Access to ECFS (Ekantik Cash Flow Strategy) and EPIG 500 (Enduring Principal Income & Growth 500) strategy education, case studies, and implementation roadmaps
  • Scorecard-based fee protection (see Section 7)
  • Priority path to BBD Chassis readiness (PPLI wrapper, premium financing, diversification compliance, guardrails infrastructure) when Member is ready
  • Quarterly strategy reviews and performance reporting
  • Priority access to ECA advisors and first-look at new features/infrastructure

NOT INCLUDED: Custody, trading, execution fees (paid to broker/custodian); premium financing interest (paid to lender); PPLI carrier fees (paid to insurance company); tax, legal, or accounting services (Member retains own advisors).

5. TERM & TERMINATION

The Founding Member relationship continues annually until terminated by either party with 60 days' written notice. Fee credit/waiver protections apply for each completed annual period during which Founding Member status is active and capacity caps remain unfilled.

6. PRICING

Founding Members pay an AUM-based flat annual program/advisory fee. The rate card is provided during onboarding and remains locked for the duration of Founding Member status (i.e., until capacity caps are reached).

  • No percentage of performance fees
  • No hidden fees or charges beyond disclosed AUM-based annual rate
  • Transparent, institutional pricing locked until capacity caps are filled

7. SCORECARD & FEE CREDIT/WAIVER POLICY

7.1 Scorecard Defined:

Each Founding Member receives a written Scorecard addendum defining annual performance objectives, measurement methodology, and time periods for ECFS and EPIG 500 allocations. Example objectives (not guarantees):

  • EPIG 500: Market participation in up markets (e.g., capture 70%+ of S&P 500 upside); capital preservation / materially smaller drawdowns in down markets (e.g., -5% vs -20% S&P)
  • ECFS: Target 2% annual yield (cash flow distributed net of fees); capital stability (NAV volatility target)

Benchmark bands, measurement methods, and definitions are set forth in the Scorecard addendum signed at onboarding.

7.2 Fee Credit/Waiver:

At the end of each annual period, ECA measures actual performance against Scorecard objectives. If objectives are not met:

  • ECA will credit or waive the next year's program/advisory fee
  • The credit/waiver applies only to ECA's program fee; it does NOT refund investment losses, custody fees, trading fees, premium financing interest, or PPLI carrier charges
  • The credit/waiver is prospective (next year's fee), not a refund of the prior year's fee or investment losses
